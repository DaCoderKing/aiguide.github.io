Now, in this article, we will talk about ChatGPT, not the AI picture generators; that's a story for another time. How exactly it works is by using tokens, usually fractions of words. However, for now, I will use full words as tokens for the sake of simplicity. If you ask it, for example, "Give me a list of ideas," it won't interpret them as words; it needs to change it into its own language, binary. In fact, all coding languages are essentially just remixes of binary code (1s and 0s), and the computer translates each to binary. Anyway, back to ChatGPT, it does the same thing. First, it breaks it down into tokens, then it breaks down those tokens into its own binary code. Then it understands like that.

With this, we have only scratched the ice. There are still so many more questions, which I will talk about. Now, one of them may or may not be, how in the world can it tell which meaning of a word you're talking about? If you don't understand what I mean, then take this as an example. There are a few meanings to "jam," the slang, meaning dancing, the food and then jammed as in stuck. If you ask it "I wanna jam, what song should I jam to?" It will look for words around it to find the correct one--which it does every time--so basically it uses grammar. The token "wanna" before "jam" shows it can't be food jam, it doesn't make sense. Then, it sees the token "song" relates it to dancing, which is one of the meanings of "jam." So, it figures out that you mean dance. What if you just wanted to share your feelings, saying just, "I wanna jam." Well, then, it will use the one that makes the most sense. Who would wanna make something stuck? Exactly. Speaking of input, what about its counterpart, output. Well, what it does is basically get the text, then pick the next word based on the last one. It actually has its own tokens, with chances of being picked. Let's go back to the jam example. You wanna jam as in dance? It figured that out already. Now, there are a few tokens it can start with. Let's say "if," "sure," or "this." (Remember, in this case we are using full words, unlike the real AI ChatGPT model.) It has different chances for different tokens. For example, "If" could have a 30% chance of being picked, with the others being uneven. If it picks "if" as its first word, great! Now, it sees the last word, "if," and the input, then repeats. If it's finding the third word, it'll see the second and the input. So the second token has chances of being "you," "that's," and more. This is called "greedy search," which isn't so effective and has a good chance of the AI slipping up.

In addition, there's another tactic that's much more effective called beam search. Beam search makes the AI look at the response as a whole, rather than one word at a time. Instead of finding the first, second, and third tokens one at a time, instead of going "If," look at the input and the last word, and make a new one. Instead, it goes "If," looks at the input and all the words before it, and then finds a word.
